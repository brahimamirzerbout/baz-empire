# 06 — Claude C. Hopkins

> Scientific Advertising (1923). The original test-and-measure operator.
> BAZ lens trigger: **"Can I prove this with a number?"**

## Bio [fact]

- **Born** 24 April 1866, Hillsdale, Michigan
- **Died** September 1932, Spring Lake, Michigan (aged 66)
- Worked: Bissell Carpet Sweeper Co., Swift & Company, Dr. Shoop's patent medicine
- 1907 (age 41): hired by **Albert Lasker** at Lord & Thomas at **$185,000/yr**
  (~$6.4M in 2025 dollars) — the legendary copywriter gig of the era
- Finished career as president & chairman of Lord & Thomas
- Inducted Advertising Hall of Fame, 1957
- David Ogilvy: *"Nobody, at any level, should be allowed to have anything to do with
  advertising until he has read this book seven times."*

## Signature work [fact]

- **Schlitz beer** — "the beer that made Milwaukee famous" + detailing the brewing
  process (plate-glass cooling rooms, white-wood-pulp filters, 4,000-ft well water,
  1,018 experiments). Took Schlitz from 5th to 1st. The reason-why copy archetype.
- **Pepsodent** — credited with popularizing tooth-brushing in America (Charles Duhigg,
  *The Power of Habit*)
- **Palmolive, Van Camp, Bissell, six different car brands**
- Invented: **test marketing, sampling by coupon, copy research, keyed advertising**

## Core principles [fact] — *Scientific Advertising* (1923)

1. **Advertising is salesmanship, multiplied.** Judge every ad by the salesman's standard:
  "Would this help me sell face-to-face?" Fine writing is a disadvantage — it reveals
  the hook.
2. **Offer service, not a buy.** "The best ads ask no one to buy." Offer wanted
   information, samples, advantages. People are selfish — they seek service for
   themselves.
3. **Keyed, traced returns.** Coupons + keyed ads → know cost per reply, cost per sale.
   "No generality has any weight." Specificity multiplies effect (78-second shave, not
   "quick shave").
4. **Test campaigns answer any question, cheaply and finally.** "Go to the court of last
   resort — the buyers." Run small, measure, scale only what wins.
5. **Headlines select the right reader.** The headline's only job: hail the people you
   can interest. "Hey there, Bill Jones."
6. **Tell the full story in one reading.** Don't serialize; readers don't come back.
   Present every important claim at once.
7. **Reason-why copy.** Explain *why* the product works — the mechanism builds belief.
8. **Negative advertising fails.** Show the bright side, not the wrinkles. Positive
   outpulls negative ~4:1.
9. **Sampling is the cheapest selling method** (when gated by interest/effort).
10. **A name that tells a story helps** (Cream of Wheat, Shredded Wheat). Coined exclusive
    names (Kodak) > generic commodity names (Toasted Corn Flakes → invites substitution).

## BAZ application

- **Hopkins is the patron saint of BAZ's "tracking is the moat."** Keyed coupons →
  server-side GTM + GA4 + CAPI + MMM-lite. Same religion, 100-year upgrade. BAZ's
  Revenue Attribution Diagnostic is a Hopkins test-campaign in modern dress.
- **"Can I prove this with a number?"** is BAZ's proof-plan trigger. Replace composites
  with real leak % + $ — Hopkins' specificity doctrine (78-second shave → "$X leaked
  per month").
- **Reason-why → mechanism:** in saturated (Stage 4-5) markets, BAZ leads with the
  *mechanism* (the tracking stack, the 90-day plan) not the claim. Pure Hopkins/Schwartz
  convergence.
- **Test campaigns before scale:** BAZ pilots the diagnostic on 3-5 clients, measures
  found-value, then scales. "From the few thousand learn what the millions will do."
- **Positive framing in BAZ copy:** sell the instrumented, forecast-able future, not the
  "your tracking is broken" fear. Bright side outpulls dark side.
